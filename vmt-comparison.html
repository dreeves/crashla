The Three Methods

1. Old Estimates (Blue) — Exponential Extrapolation

The original Google Sheet estimates projected exponential growth from mid-2025 trends without being constrained by the 200M milestone. This produced runaway growth: 28.5M/month by January 2026. Problem: at that rate, cumulative would reach ~240M by mid-February — but Waymo only hit 200M around Feb 14. These estimates are definitively too high for Oct–Jan.

2. CA-Proxy (Red) — CPUC Monthly Distribution

Uses actual California CPUC driverless VMT filings to distribute US miles within milestone intervals. CA monthly VMT is the most granular real data we have. Problem: CA VMT plateaued at ~8.4M/month in Nov–Dec 2025, and the model assumes constant CA share within each interval. This produces a US plateau (16.4M for Nov, Dec, and Jan) that contradicts all growth indicators. It also creates a spurious 33% dip in June 2025 from a CA-specific anomaly (possibly service disruptions from anti-ICE protests in LA/SF). CA is increasingly unrepresentative as Austin and other non-CA markets scale.

3. Smooth Interpolation (Green) — PCHIP Through 13 Milestones

Fits a monotone cubic curve (PCHIP) through 13 cumulative milestone data points, including the newly found 100M milestone on July 15, 2025. Monthly VMT is derived from first differences of the cumulative curve. Strengths: hits every milestone exactly, produces smooth monotonic growth, no CA-specific artifacts. Consistent with weekly trip count cross-checks (model/trip ratio 0.93–1.01x for 2025 data points). Limitation: assumes smooth growth between milestones — can’t capture real month-to-month fluctuations from events like city launches or service disruptions. But for crash-rate denominators, smooth is arguably more appropriate than noisy. Uncertainty bands (±25–30%) are calibrated to encompass the full envelope of alternative models (Akima, linear, CA-proxy) plus milestone perturbations and a margin for unmodeled events.

Verdict: The smooth model is our best estimate. It respects milestone constraints, shows continued exponential growth (~10–14%/month in late 2025), and cross-checks against independent trip count data. The CA-proxy model’s plateau and June dip are artifacts of using a single region as a proxy for national operations.

Why Not Just Use CA Data?

As Waymo expands beyond California, the CPUC data becomes a less reliable proxy for US-wide monthly patterns. Three signals illustrate this:

1. CA VMT plateaued while US growth accelerated. CA deployment VMT was 8.4M in both Nov and Dec 2025 (CPUC filings), yet Waymo’s US milestone pace was accelerating: the time to add each 25M miles dropped from ~5 months (25M→50M) to ~6 weeks (175M→200M). Non-CA markets (Austin, Phoenix highways) were driving the growth.

2. June 2025 CA dip didn’t happen nationally. CA deployment VMT dropped 33% in June (5.4M→3.6M), but the milestone data shows Waymo accumulated 25M US miles in Apr–Jun at an accelerating pace, reaching 100M just 15 days into July. The June dip was CA-specific.

3. Trip counts and weekly miles confirm smooth growth. Waymo reported 250K weekly trips (Apr), ~450K (Dec), and 400K+ (Feb 2026). At 8–9 miles/trip including deadhead, these imply 8.7M→17.5M monthly VMT — consistent with the smooth model, not the CA-proxy plateau.

4. Weekly miles rate data independently validates the curve shape. Official Waymo statements report lower-bound weekly mile rates: “1M+/week” (Oct 2024), “2M+/week” (Jul 2025), and “over 4 million miles per week” (Feb 4, 2026 — Waymo CSO Mauricio Pena, US Senate testimony). The smooth model’s PCHIP derivative gives 1.2M, 2.1M, and 4.4M/week at those dates — consistently above the stated floors, with a ratio of 1.03–1.22x. The model predicts crossing 4M/week on Dec 29, 2025. A Dec 8 CNBC report citing “2M+/week” appears to be a stale figure; the model shows 3.7M/week by then.

Search for Intermediate Milestones

The biggest gap in our data is Sep 30 (127M) to ~Feb 14 (200M) — 4.5 months and 73M miles. We exhaustively searched for any intermediate cumulative milestone from Waymo (blog posts, X/Twitter, Alphabet Q3 & Q4 2025 earnings calls, Safety Impact Hub updates, Senate testimony, SEC filings, the 2025 Year in Review blog, the $16B fundraise announcement).

Result: no intermediate cumulative milestone exists. Waymo did not announce 150M or 175M. The Q3 2025 earnings call (Oct 29) mentioned no mileage figure. The Dec 16 Safety Hub update still referenced 127M through September. The $16B blog (Feb 2) still cited “127 million miles.” The 200M figure first appeared as “nearly 200M” on Feb 4–12, then “over 200M” on Feb 23.

However, we found a valuable rate constraint: Waymo CSO Mauricio Pena testified to the US Senate on Feb 4, 2026 that Waymo drives “over 4 million miles” per week. Our model predicts 4.4M/week on that date and first crosses 4M/week on Dec 29 — perfectly consistent.

Third-party estimates (EE Times: ~150M, Driverless Digest: ~165M at year-end 2025) are lower than our model (~172M) because they extrapolated from 127M without the 200M endpoint constraint.

Uncertainty: What the Bands Do and Don’t Capture

What the bands cover

The ±25–30% bands on the smooth model are calibrated to encompass:

  • Alternative interpolation methods — PCHIP, Akima, and linear interpolation give different monthly distributions. The bands cover all three.
  • The CA-proxy methodology — an entirely different approach (distributing US miles proportionally to CA CPUC monthly data). Every CA-proxy estimate falls within the smooth model’s bands.
  • Milestone value uncertainty (±1%) — “22 million” could mean 21.8M or 22.2M. 500 Monte Carlo perturbations confirm the bands cover this.
  • 200M crossing date uncertainty (±5 days) — shifting the date from Feb 8 to Feb 20 changes Oct–Jan estimates by only ±4–9%.
  • A 10% margin for unmodeled events — service disruptions, city launches, seasonal effects, fleet maintenance.

What the bands don’t capture

  • Month-to-month correlation. Oct–Jan must sum to ~73M (milestone-constrained). If Oct is at its low end, Nov–Jan must compensate. The bands are valid per-month for crash-rate denominators, but you cannot read all four lo values simultaneously (that would imply only 44M total, not 73M). For cumulative uncertainty, the bands are tighter than they appear because errors partially cancel.
  • No independent non-CA monthly data. We have no monthly VMT data for Austin, Phoenix, or other non-CA markets. The smooth interpolation papers over this gap. If a single city had a dramatic ramp-up or shutdown in a specific month, we wouldn’t see it.
  • Jul 2025 is pathologically sensitive to the 100M milestone value. Because it straddles two tight intervals (96M on Jun 30 → 100M on Jul 15 → 127M on Sep 30), a ±2% shift in the 100M milestone moves Jul’s estimate by ±34%. The ±25% band just barely covers this.
  • Trip count cross-checks have their own uncertainty. Waymo’s “150K+” and “2M+” figures are lower bounds, and converting trips to miles requires assuming a miles-per-trip ratio (6.7–9.0) that itself changes over time.

Sensitivity analysis summary

Combined worst-case perturbations (127M→125M, 200M date ±6 days, value ±3M) only shift monthly estimates by ±2–4%. The ±30% bands are dominated by interpolation shape uncertainty (which growth trajectory is correct between milestones), not milestone precision. For crash-rate analysis, this is the honest level of uncertainty: we know the cumulative total well, but the month-by-month distribution within the Oct–Feb interval is genuinely uncertain at the ±30% level.
